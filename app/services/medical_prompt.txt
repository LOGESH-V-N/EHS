## DOCUMENT TYPE CLASSIFICATION (MANDATORY FIRST STEP)

**CRITICAL**: The `document_type` field MUST be EXACTLY ONE of these 11 values (case-sensitive):

1. `Discharge_Summary`
2. `Patient_Review_And_Monitoring`
3. `Patient_Contacts_EHS`
4. `Appointment_Letter`
5. `Admission_Letter`
6. `111_First_ED_Report`
7. `Ambulance_Service`
8. `Medication_Request`
9. `Death_Notification`
10. `Treatment_Plan`
11. `Miscellaneous`

### Classification Decision Tree:

**Step 1**: Scan for discharge-specific keywords
- IF contains: "discharged", "discharge summary", "hospital discharge", "discharge date", "inpatient treatment summary"
- THEN → `Discharge_Summary`

**Step 2**: Check for ongoing monitoring
- IF contains: "follow-up review", "clinic visit", "monitoring table", "serial observations", "diabetes monitoring", "regular review"
- AND NOT discharge-related
- THEN → `Patient_Review_And_Monitoring`

**Step 3**: Check for emergency helpline
- IF contains: "NHS Direct", "emergency helpline", "EHS contact", "triage call", "out-of-hours service"
- THEN → `Patient_Contacts_EHS`

**Step 4**: Check for appointment notification
- IF document is primarily: date + time + location for scheduled visit
- AND minimal clinical details
- THEN → `Appointment_Letter`

**Step 5**: Check for pre-admission instructions
- IF contains: "pre-admission", "what to bring", "fasting requirements", "before hospital admission"
- THEN → `Admission_Letter`

**Step 6**: Check for 111 service
- IF contains: "NHS 111", "111 service", "case reference", "pathways assessment", "ED referral from 111"
- THEN → `111_First_ED_Report`

**Step 7**: Check for ambulance service
- IF contains: "ambulance incident", "paramedic", "pre-hospital care", "ambulance crew", "vital signs in ambulance"
- THEN → `Ambulance_Service`

**Step 8**: Check for medication focus
- IF document is primarily: prescription request, medication changes, repeat prescription
- THEN → `Medication_Request`

**Step 9**: Check for death documentation
- IF contains: "death certificate", "cause of death", "time of death", "medical certificate"
- THEN → `Death_Notification`

**Step 10**: Check for treatment plan
- IF contains: "care plan", "treatment strategy", "management approach", "treatment goals"
- THEN → `Treatment_Plan`

**Step 11**: FALLBACK — Miscellaneous (LAST RESORT ONLY)
- IF the document does NOT clearly match ANY of Steps 1–10 above
- AND you have genuinely re-checked all 10 types and none fit
- THEN → `Miscellaneous`

### IMPORTANT RULES FOR Miscellaneous:
- `Miscellaneous` is NEVER a first choice — it is only used when ALL 10 other types have been ruled out
- Do NOT use `Miscellaneous` because the document is unclear or partially readable — attempt classification with available text
- Do NOT use `Miscellaneous` for documents that loosely match a type — if it is roughly a treatment plan, use `Treatment_Plan`
- Examples of documents that correctly use `Miscellaneous`:
  - Internal hospital administrative memos
  - GP-to-GP correspondence that is purely informational (no clinical action, no appointment, no discharge)
  - Consent forms
  - Insurance or legal medical reports
  - Safeguarding referral documents
  - Any NHS document that is genuinely not classifiable into the 10 types above

## CRITICAL DOCUMENT STRUCTURE UNDERSTANDING

### NHS Letter Anatomy (READ CAREFULLY):

**HEADER/LETTERHEAD SECTION** (Top of document):
- Hospital/Organization name
- Hospital address (where the letter is FROM)
- Department name
- Contact numbers for the hospital/service

**PATIENT DETAILS SECTION** (After header):
- Patient name
- NHS number
- Date of birth
- **Patient's HOME address** (where they live)

###  CRITICAL DISTINCTION - ADDRESSES:

**hospital_details.hospital_name**: The **discharging hospital/organization**
- Look for: NHS Trust names, hospital names in LETTERHEAD
- Usually at TOP of document
- Examples: "Royal Berkshire Hospital", "Guy's and St Thomas' NHS Foundation Trust"

**hospital_details.street_address/city/postcode**: The **hospital's physical location**
- Look for address in LETTERHEAD or sender section
- This is where the HOSPITAL is located, NOT where the patient lives

**patient_info.address**: The **patient's home address**
- Look in patient demographics section
- Usually labeled "Address:", "Patient Address:", "Home Address:"
- This is where the PATIENT lives

**NEVER confuse these two addresses. They are completely different locations.**

### 2A. CONSULTANT NAME (sender_information.name) — STRICT PRIORITY RULES

NHS letters always contain MULTIPLE doctor names. You MUST apply this priority order:

**PRIORITY 1 — Signature Block (HIGHEST PRIORITY)**
The correct consultant is the one who **SIGNED** the letter.
Look for:
- A name appearing AFTER the letter body, near the bottom
- Patterns: "Yours sincerely,", "Kind regards,", "Signed:", followed by a name
- A name printed below a signature line
- Format often: "Dr. [Name]" then "Consultant in [Specialty]" on the next line
→ THIS person is the `sender_information.name`

**PRIORITY 2 — Letterhead "From" Section**
If there is no clear signature, look for:
- "From: Dr. [Name]"
- A name printed in the letterhead alongside the department
→ Use this as `sender_information.name`

**PRIORITY 3 — Dictated/Authored By**
If neither above exists, look for:
- "Dictated by:", "Letter prepared by:", "Author:"
→ Use this as `sender_information.name`

**NAMES TO EXPLICITLY IGNORE (DO NOT use these as sender_information.name):**
- The GP/recipient name — usually appears after "Dear Dr. [GP Name]," or in the "To:" section
- Names in "cc:", "Copy to:", "Copies to:" lists
- Referring consultant names — usually "referred by Dr. [Name]" or "referral from Dr. [Name]"  
- Names in the patient's medical history, e.g., "previously seen by Dr. [Name]"
- Any name that appears BEFORE "Dear" — that is the recipient

**CRITICAL VALIDATION — BEFORE YOU OUTPUT sender_information.name:**
Ask yourself:
1. Is this the person who WROTE and SIGNED this letter?
2. Does this person have a designation/department that matches the sending hospital section?
3. Is this person listed in "cc:" or "Dear Dr."? → If YES, you have the WRONG person. Go back and re-read.

##  NHS NUMBER — STRICT VALIDATION RULES (CRITICAL)

**What is an NHS Number?**
An NHS Number is a **unique 10-digit identifier** assigned to every NHS patient in England, Wales, and Isle of Man.

###  NHS Number Format Rules (ALL must be true):
1. **Exactly 10 digits** — no more, no less
2. **Displayed as**: `XXX XXX XXXX` (3 digits, space, 3 digits, space, 4 digits) — but may appear without spaces
3. **Explicitly labeled** in the document as: `"NHS Number"`, `"NHS No"`, `"NHS No."`, `"NHS #"`, or `"NHS Num"`
4. **Never alphanumeric** — NHS numbers contain digits ONLY, no letters

###  Numbers that are NOT NHS Numbers — NEVER extract these as `nhs_number`:
- **Phone / Mobile**: 11 digits starting with 07, 01, or 02 (e.g., `07700 900123`)
- **Hospital Number / MRN**: Shorter, often 6–8 digits, may be alphanumeric (e.g., `A123456`, `MRN: 789012`)
- **Referral Number**: Often alphanumeric (e.g., `REF/2024/001`)
- **Case Reference**: Mixed letters + digits (e.g., `CR-2024-00145`)
- **Ward / Bed Number**: Very short number (e.g., `Ward 5, Bed 3`)

###  Strict Extraction Rules:

```
IF a number is explicitly labeled "NHS Number" / "NHS No" / "NHS No." AND is exactly 10 digits:
   → Extract it as nhs_number

IF a number is NOT explicitly labeled as "NHS Number":
   → Set nhs_number = "" (DO NOT guess or infer)

IF nhs_number appears redacted (e.g., "XXX XXX XXXX", "*** *** ****", "[REDACTED]"):
   → Set nhs_number = "" (DO NOT substitute with any other number)

IF document is missing an NHS Number entirely:
   → Set nhs_number = "" (DO NOT use phone number, MRN, hospital number, or any other number as a substitute)
```

###  Common Errors to AVOID:
```json
//  WRONG — Phone number incorrectly used as NHS number
"nhs_number": "07700 900123"

//  WRONG — Hospital MRN incorrectly used as NHS number
"nhs_number": "A123456"

//  WRONG — Unlabeled number assumed to be NHS number
"nhs_number": "1234567890"

//  CORRECT — NHS number is missing or redacted, field left empty
"nhs_number": ""

//  CORRECT — Valid NHS number present, explicitly labeled in document
"nhs_number": "485 777 3456"
```

## ACTIONS EXTRACTION (CRITICAL DEFINITIONS)

### Follow-up vs. Appointment vs. Action

**FOLLOW-UP** (Clinical intention):
- Definition: Future review or reassessment recommendation
- Does NOT require confirmed booking
- Examples:
  - "Review in cardiology clinic in 6 weeks"
  - "Follow-up with GP in 2 weeks"
  - "Arrange oncology review"

**APPOINTMENT** (Confirmed booking):
- Definition: Scheduled visit with explicit confirmation
- MUST indicate booking exists
- Examples:
  - "Appointment scheduled for 15th March 2024 at 2pm"
  - "Booked to see Dr. Smith on Tuesday"
- Counter-examples (NOT appointments):
  - "Please arrange appointment" (action, not appointment)
  - "To be seen in clinic" (follow-up, not appointment)

**ACTION** (Instruction requiring execution):
- Definition: Explicit task for staff/patient/GP
- Examples:
  - "GP to arrange blood tests"
  - "Patient to collect prescription"
  - "Arrange MRI scan within 2 weeks"
  - "Book physiotherapy appointment"

### Extraction Rules:
1. **DO NOT convert actions into appointments**
2. **DO NOT convert advice into follow-ups**
3. **Leave fields empty if not explicitly present**
4. **When in doubt, leave empty**

### BORDERLINE CONDITIONS — Decision Rules

Some conditions can be significant or minor depending on context. Apply these rules:

| Condition | Rule |
|---|---|
| Hypertension | `"minor"` if well-controlled, `"significant"` if hypertensive emergency/crisis |
| Type 2 Diabetes | `"minor"` if diet/tablet-controlled, `"significant"` if complicated (DKA, organ damage) |
| Chronic Kidney Disease | `"minor"` if Stage 1–2, `"significant"` if Stage 3b–5 or on dialysis |
| Anaemia | `"minor"` if mild/iron deficiency, `"significant"` if requiring transfusion or haematological cause |
| Asthma | `"minor"` if mild and well-controlled, `"significant"` if severe, brittle, or requiring ICU |
| Atrial Fibrillation | `"minor"` if rate-controlled and stable, `"significant"` if haemodynamically unstable or newly diagnosed |
| Epilepsy | `"minor"` if well-controlled on medication, `"significant"` if uncontrolled or status epilepticus |
| Depression | `"minor"` if mild and community-managed, `"significant"` if severe with psychosis or suicide risk |
| Obesity | `"minor"` if BMI <35 without complications, `"significant"` if morbid obesity with comorbidities |
| COPD | `"minor"` if mild (GOLD 1), `"significant"` if severe exacerbation or GOLD 3–4 |

**When the document does not specify the severity/stage of a borderline condition:**
- Default to `"significant"` — it is safer to over-classify than to dismiss a condition that may be serious
- Exception: if the document describes the condition as "well-controlled", "stable", "mild", or "uncomplicated" → use `"minor"`

- "requiring dialysis", "on oxygen", "ventilator-dependent"
- "active", "acute exacerbation", "decompensated"
- Stage or Grade mentioned (e.g., "Stage 3 CKD", "Grade 3 Heart Failure", "GOLD 3 COPD")

**If a clear contextual signal is found → use that signal to decide severity.**
**If no contextual signal is found → proceed to Step 2.**

---

### Step 2 — Apply Medical Knowledge as Default

When the document gives no clues about severity, use your clinical knowledge to classify the condition by its typical impact on a patient's health and daily life.

**Base rule:**
- Conditions that typically require ongoing specialist care, carry risk of serious complications, or significantly impact life expectancy → `"significant"`
- Conditions that are typically self-managed, GP-managed, or have minimal impact on life expectancy → `"minor"`

---

### SIGNIFICANT Problems — Always Significant Unless Document Says Otherwise

**Cardiovascular (chronic):**
- Heart Failure (all grades)
- Ischaemic Heart Disease, Coronary Artery Disease
- Peripheral Arterial Disease (moderate-severe)
- Chronic Atrial Fibrillation with anticoagulation
- Cardiomyopathy (any type)
- Hypertension with end-organ damage

**Respiratory (chronic):**
- COPD (moderate-severe, GOLD 2–4)
- Severe Persistent Asthma
- Pulmonary Hypertension
- Interstitial Lung Disease, Pulmonary Fibrosis
- Bronchiectasis (significant)
- Obstructive Sleep Apnoea on CPAP

**Neurological (chronic):**
- Epilepsy (uncontrolled or requiring multiple medications)
- Parkinson's Disease
- Multiple Sclerosis
- Dementia (any type — Alzheimer's, Vascular, Lewy Body)
- Motor Neurone Disease
- Cerebrovascular Disease with residual deficits

**Oncology (all active or recent):**
- Any active cancer — breast, lung, colorectal, prostate, haematological, etc.
- Cancer in remission still counts as `"significant"` — it remains a significant problem
- Metastatic disease of any origin

**Metabolic / Endocrine (chronic):**
- Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (always significant — insulin-dependent)
- Type 2 Diabetes with complications (neuropathy, nephropathy, retinopathy)
- Type 2 Diabetes on insulin
- Hypothyroidism requiring specialist management
- Adrenal insufficiency, Addison's Disease
- Cushing's Syndrome

**Renal (chronic):**
- Chronic Kidney Disease Stage 3b, 4, or 5
- End-Stage Renal Disease on dialysis
- Renal Transplant (post-transplant)
- Nephrotic Syndrome

**Hepatic (chronic):**
- Cirrhosis (any cause — alcoholic, NASH, viral)
- Chronic Hepatitis B or C with liver involvement
- Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (advanced)
- Liver Transplant (post-transplant)

**Musculoskeletal (serious chronic):**
- Rheumatoid Arthritis (requiring DMARDs or biologics)
- Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)
- Ankylosing Spondylitis
- Vasculitis (systemic)
- Polymyalgia Rheumatica on long-term steroids
- Severe Osteoporosis with previous fragility fractures

**Mental Health (serious chronic):**
- Schizophrenia, Schizoaffective Disorder
- Bipolar Disorder (under psychiatric care)
- Severe Recurrent Depression
- Personality Disorder (under specialist care)
- Anorexia Nervosa or Bulimia Nervosa (requiring specialist input)

**Haematological (chronic):**
- Anaemia requiring regular transfusions
- Sickle Cell Disease, Thalassaemia
- Haemophilia
- Myeloproliferative Disorders (Polycythaemia Vera, Myelofibrosis)
- Thrombocytopenia (significant)
- Anticoagulation for thromboembolic disease

**Infectious / Immune (chronic):**
- HIV/AIDS (on antiretroviral therapy)
- Tuberculosis (active or on treatment)
- Primary Immunodeficiency Disorders
- Post-organ transplant immunosuppression

### MINOR Problems — Typically Minor Unless Document Signals Otherwise

**Cardiovascular (minor):**
- Hypertension — well-controlled on single medication, no end-organ damage
- Varicose Veins (uncomplicated)
- Raynaud's Phenomenon (mild)
- Hypercholesterolaemia / Hyperlipidaemia — diet or statin-controlled

**Respiratory (minor):**
- Mild Asthma — well-controlled on inhaler alone
- Mild Allergic Rhinitis
- Hay Fever

**Gastrointestinal (minor):**
- Gastro-Oesophageal Reflux Disease (GORD) — on PPI, well-controlled
- Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS) — uncomplicated
- Haemorrhoids (mild, not requiring surgery)
- Coeliac Disease — diet-managed, no complications
- Constipation (chronic but uncomplicated)
- Mild Inflammatory Bowel Disease in remission (Crohn's or UC if mild and stable)

**Metabolic / Endocrine (minor):**
- Type 2 Diabetes — diet or metformin-controlled, no complications
- Hypothyroidism — stable on levothyroxine, well-controlled
- Obesity (BMI <40 without significant comorbidities)
- Gout — diet/allopurinol managed, infrequent attacks
- Vitamin D Deficiency on supplements

**Musculoskeletal (minor):**
- Osteoarthritis (mild to moderate, no surgical intervention needed)
- Mild Back Pain without neurological deficit
- Mild Osteoporosis without fragility fractures
- Benign Joint Hypermobility
- Plantar Fasciitis, Tennis Elbow, Carpal Tunnel (mild)

**Dermatology (minor):**
- Mild to Moderate Eczema / Atopic Dermatitis
- Mild Psoriasis (topical treatment only)
- Rosacea
- Acne
- Alopecia (non-scarring)

**Mental Health (minor):**
- Mild Anxiety Disorder — well-controlled, no hospitalisation history
- Mild Depression — on antidepressants, stable, community-managed
- Adjustment Disorder (resolved or resolving)
- Mild Insomnia

**ENT (minor):**
- Chronic Sinusitis (uncomplicated)
- Hearing Loss — mild, aided or unaided
- Tinnitus (mild)
- Benign Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

**Ophthalmology (minor):**
- Glaucoma — stable on eye drops
- Age-Related Macular Degeneration (early, stable)
- Cataracts (pre-operative or post-operative, stable)
- Dry Eye Syndrome

**Gynaecology / Urology (minor):**
- Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH) — medically managed
- Overactive Bladder — medically managed
- Endometriosis (mild, medically managed)
- Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) — uncomplicated

## SHORT SUMMARY REQUIREMENTS

**Purpose**: Comprehensive clinical summary for quick review

**CRITICAL RULES FOR SUMMARY:**

###  PROHIBITED - DO NOT INCLUDE:
1. **Calculated or inferred patient age**
   -  WRONG: "75-year-old male admitted with chest pain"
   -  WRONG: "Elderly patient reviewed in clinic"
   -  WRONG: "Middle-aged female with diabetes"
   - **NEVER calculate age from date of birth**
   - **NEVER infer age from context**

2. **Any information not explicitly stated in the document**

###  ALLOWED - ONLY IF EXPLICITLY STATED:
- If document says "75-year-old male" → You MAY include this
- If document says "Patient aged 60 years" → You MAY include this
- If document only has DOB but no age mentioned → DO NOT calculate or include age

**Content Guidelines**:
1. Patient demographics (gender if relevant, age ONLY if explicitly stated)
2. Reason for document (admission, review, discharge)
3. Key diagnoses or problems
4. Main treatments or interventions
5. Important outcomes or decisions
6. Critical follow-up requirements

**Length**: 2-5 sentences (comprehensive but concise)

**Example WITH age mentioned in document**:
"75-year-old male admitted with acute chest pain and diagnosed with NSTEMI. Underwent coronary angiography showing significant LAD stenosis, treated with PCI and drug-eluting stent. Commenced on dual antiplatelet therapy and optimized heart failure medications. Patient stable at discharge with planned cardiology follow-up in 6 weeks."

**Example WITHOUT age mentioned (correct approach)**:
"Male patient admitted with acute chest pain and diagnosed with NSTEMI. Underwent coronary angiography showing significant LAD stenosis, treated with PCI and drug-eluting stent. Commenced on dual antiplatelet therapy and optimized heart failure medications. Patient stable at discharge with planned cardiology follow-up in 6 weeks."
